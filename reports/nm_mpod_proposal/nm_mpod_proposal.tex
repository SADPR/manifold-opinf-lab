\title{\textbf{Nonlinear-Map Manifold POD Operator Inference}\\
\large A nonintrusive closure-map extension of polynomial-manifold OpInf}
\author{Draft manuscript}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Polynomial-manifold Operator Inference (OpInf) enriches a low-dimensional
linear modal approximation by expressing secondary modal coordinates as
polynomial functions of the primary coordinates.  This work considers the
corresponding nonlinear-map construction
\[
  \bu(t;\bmu)\approx \uref+\bV\bq(t;\bmu)
  +\bVbar\Nmap(\bq(t;\bmu),\bmu),
\]
where the secondary coordinate map $\Nmap$ is learned from data by a Gaussian
process or neural network.  The construction is motivated by intrusive
closure-map projection methods, but is placed here in the nonintrusive OpInf
setting, where the reduced vector-field library must be specified explicitly
from data.

For quadratic full-order dynamics, the modal substitution shows that the
quadratically complete reduced library in the augmented coordinates
$[\bq^T,\bz^T]^T$, $\bz=\Nmap(\bq,\bmu)$, contains
$\bq\otimes\bq$, $\bq\otimes\bz$, and $\bz\otimes\bz$.  We compare this
quadratic-complete model with two cheaper nested alternatives: an
augmented-linear model in $[\bq,\bz]$ and a primary-quadratic model that keeps
$\bq\otimes\bq$ but omits the mixed and secondary quadratic blocks.  Numerical
tests on a Korteweg--de Vries benchmark and a parametric inviscid Burgers
problem show that the learned nonlinear map can greatly improve accuracy over
linear-subspace OpInf and polynomial MPOD.  The Burgers results also show a
clear tradeoff: the quadratic-complete library is the most accurate but, in its
uncompressed form, is too expensive online; the augmented-linear variant gives
the best accuracy-cost compromise in the present experiments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Projection-based reduced-order models are most effective when the solution
manifold is well approximated by a low-dimensional linear subspace.  For
transport-dominated problems this assumption can be restrictive: shocks,
fronts, and coherent waves often lead to slowly decaying Kolmogorov
$n$-widths.  Nonlinear-manifold reduced models address this issue by replacing
a purely linear approximation with a low-dimensional nonlinear embedding.

Geelen, Balzano, Wright, and Willcox proposed a nonintrusive OpInf framework in
which the nonlinear manifold has polynomial modal structure
\begin{equation}
  \bs(t)\approx \bs_{\mathrm{ref}}
  +\bV\widehat{\bs}(t)+\bVbar\bXi\boldsymbol g_p(\widehat{\bs}(t)),
  \label{eq:willcox_decoder}
\end{equation}
and the reduced operators are inferred from data after deriving the algebraic
structure induced by this manifold \cite{geelen2024learning}.  This paper asks
a closely related question: can the polynomial secondary-coordinate map in
\eqref{eq:willcox_decoder} be replaced by a learned nonlinear map while
preserving the OpInf structure?

The question is also connected to closure-map projection methods.  Quadratic
manifold projection \cite{barnett2022quadratic}, neural-network-augmented
projection \cite{barnett2023neural}, and GPR/RBF latent-space closure models
\cite{aresdeparga2025closure} all exploit the idea that unresolved modal
coordinates can be predicted from resolved coordinates.  Those methods are
intrusive: after the closure map is chosen, the governing equations can be
projected directly.  In nonintrusive OpInf the full residual is not evaluated,
so the induced reduced library must be selected explicitly.  The main
contribution here is to spell out that library for a learned nonlinear
secondary map and to test the resulting models on two benchmarks.

The remainder of the manuscript follows the structure of
\cite{geelen2024learning}.  Section~\ref{sec:method} derives the nonlinear-map
manifold and the associated OpInf libraries.  Section~\ref{sec:benchmarks}
defines the KdV and Burgers problems.  Section~\ref{sec:results} presents a
compact numerical comparison.  Appendix~\ref{app:audit} collects
supplementary diagnostics: feature counts, online timings, an extrapolatory
visualization, and the MPOD order sweep.

\section{Nonlinear-map manifold OpInf}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{State approximation}

Let $\bu(t;\bmu)\in\RR^N$ denote the semidiscrete full-order state.  We split a
POD basis into primary and secondary modes,
\[
  \bV\in\RR^{N\times \qdim},\qquad
  \bVbar\in\RR^{N\times\rbar},\qquad
  \bV^T\bVbar=\bzero,
\]
and approximate the state by
\begin{equation}
  \bu(t;\bmu)
  \approx
  \uref+\bV\bq(t;\bmu)+\bVbar\bz(t;\bmu),
  \qquad
  \bz(t;\bmu)=\Nmap(\bq(t;\bmu),\bmu).
  \label{eq:nm_decoder}
\end{equation}
Only $\bq(t;\bmu)\in\RR^{\qdim}$ is evolved online.  The secondary coordinates
$\bz\in\RR^{\rbar}$ are recovered algebraically.  In this sense
\eqref{eq:nm_decoder} is a nonlinear-map analogue of MPOD: MPOD uses
$\bz=\bXi\boldsymbol g_p(\bq)$, whereas NM-MPOD uses a learned map.

\subsection{Quadratic projected algebra}

Consider first a quadratic semidiscrete model without explicit parameter
notation,
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\bu}=\bc+\bA\bu+\bH(\bu\otimes\bu).
  \label{eq:quadratic_fom}
\end{equation}
For a linear POD decoder, $\bu\approx\uref+\bV\bq$, substituting into
\eqref{eq:quadratic_fom} and projecting with $\bV^T$ yields
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\bq}=\widehat{\bc}+\widehat{\bA}\bq
  +\widehat{\bH}(\bq\otimes\bq).
  \label{eq:linear_opinf_structure}
\end{equation}
This is the standard quadratic OpInf library.

For the nonlinear-map decoder \eqref{eq:nm_decoder}, the chain rule gives
\[
  \dot{\bu}
  =
  \bV\dot{\bq}
  +
  \bVbar
  \frac{\partial \Nmap}{\partial\bq}(\bq,\bmu)\dot{\bq}.
\]
Under the modal projection used in MPOD-OpInf, the tangent contribution
vanishes because $\bV^T\bVbar=\bzero$, so $\bV^T\dot{\bu}=\dot{\bq}$.  The
right-hand side, however, changes.  Expanding the quadratic term gives
\begin{align}
  \bu\otimes\bu
  ={}&
  \uref\otimes\uref
  +\uref\otimes\bV\bq
  +\bV\bq\otimes\uref
  +\uref\otimes\bVbar\bz
  +\bVbar\bz\otimes\uref
  \notag\\
  &+
  \bV\bq\otimes\bV\bq
  +\bV\bq\otimes\bVbar\bz
  +\bVbar\bz\otimes\bV\bq
  +\bVbar\bz\otimes\bVbar\bz.
  \label{eq:nm_expansion}
\end{align}
Thus the reduced dynamics have the split form
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\bq}
  =
  \bc_0+\bA_q\bq+\bA_z\bz
  +\bH_{qq}(\bq\otimes\bq)
  +\bH_{qz}(\bq\otimes\bz)
  +\bH_{zz}(\bz\otimes\bz),
  \qquad
  \bz=\Nmap(\bq,\bmu).
  \label{eq:qc_structure}
\end{equation}
The mixed and secondary quadratic blocks are therefore not optional from the
viewpoint of algebraic completeness.  They may be regularized, compressed, or
removed as model approximations, but their origin is the quadratic full-order
vector field combined with the split decoder.

sufficient.

\subsection{Nonintrusive regressions}

Let $\bq_j=\bV^T(\bu_j-\uref)$ and
$\qbar_j=\bVbar^T(\bu_j-\uref)$ be the primary and secondary coordinates of the
training snapshots.  A nonlinear secondary map is fit by solving, for example,
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol\psi^\star
  =
  \argmin_{\boldsymbol\psi}
  \sum_{j=1}^{M}
  \left\|
  \qbar_j-\Nmap_{\boldsymbol\psi}(\bq_j,\bmu_j)
  \right\|_2^2
  +\alpha\|\boldsymbol\psi\|_2^2
  \label{eq:ann_fit}
\end{equation}
for a neural network, or by maximizing the marginal likelihood when
$\Nmap$ is represented by a Gaussian process.

Once the decoder is fixed, the reduced dynamics are inferred by continuous-time
OpInf.  With
\[
  \bTheta=
  \begin{bmatrix}
  \btheta(\bq_1,\bmu_1)&\cdots&\btheta(\bq_M,\bmu_M)
  \end{bmatrix},
  \qquad
  \bdotQ=
  \begin{bmatrix}
  \dot{\bq}_1&\cdots&\dot{\bq}_M
  \end{bmatrix},
\]
the regularized regression is
\begin{equation}
  \bW^\star
  =
  \argmin_{\bW}
  \left\|
  \bdotQ-\bW\bTheta
  \right\|_F^2
  +
  \left\|
  \bW\bLambda
  \right\|_F^2.
  \label{eq:opinf_fit}
\end{equation}
The online ROM is then
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\bq}=\bW^\star\btheta(\bq,\bmu),
  \qquad
  \bu_{\mathrm{ROM}}=\uref+\bV\bq+\bVbar\Nmap(\bq,\bmu).
  \label{eq:rom}
\end{equation}

\section{Benchmark problems}
\label{sec:benchmarks}

\subsection{Korteweg--de Vries equation}

The first test is the periodic Korteweg--de Vries equation
\begin{equation}
  s_t+\alpha s s_x+\beta s_{xxx}=0,
  \qquad x\in[-\pi,\pi),\qquad t\in[0,1],
  \label{eq:kdv}
\end{equation}
with $\alpha=4$, $\beta=1$, periodic boundary conditions, and
\[
  s(x,0)=1+24\,\mathrm{sech}^2(\sqrt{8}\,x).
\]
The full model uses a Fourier pseudospectral discretization with $N=256$ grid
points and exponential time differencing Runge--Kutta time integration with
$\Delta t=2\times10^{-4}$.  The interval $t\in[0,0.2]$ is used for training and
$t\in(0.2,1]$ for prediction.

\subsection{Parametric inviscid Burgers equation}

The second test is the parametric inviscid Burgers problem on $x\in[0,100]$,
\begin{equation}
  u_t+\left(\frac{u^2}{2}\right)_x=0.02\exp(\mu_2 x),
  \qquad
  u(x,0)=1,
  \label{eq:burgers}
\end{equation}
with left inflow flux $f(u(0,t))=\mu_1^2/2$ and
\[
  \bmu=(\mu_1,\mu_2)^T,\qquad
  \mu_1\in[4.25,5.50],\qquad
  \mu_2\in[0.015,0.030].
\]
The high-dimensional model uses $N=5000$ finite-volume cells and backward Euler
time integration with $\Delta t=0.05$ for 500 steps.  The nonintrusive
parametric library uses
\begin{equation}
  \boldeta(\bmu)=
  \begin{bmatrix}
  \mu_1 & \mu_2 & \mu_1^2 & \mu_1\mu_2 & \mu_2^2
  \end{bmatrix}^T.
  \label{eq:param_features}
\end{equation}
Thus, for example, the Burgers QC library is
\[
  \btheta_{\mathrm{QC}}(\bq,\bmu)=
  \begin{bmatrix}
  1\\
  \boldeta(\bmu)\\
  \bq\\
  \boldeta(\bmu)\otimes\bq\\
  \bq_{\mathrm{quad}}\\
  \bz\\
  \bq\otimes\bz\\
  \bz_{\mathrm{quad}}
  \end{bmatrix},
  \qquad
  \bz=\Nmap(\bq,\bmu).
\]

Training uses the $3\times3$ parameter grid
\[
  \mu_1\in\{4.25,4.875,5.50\},\qquad
  \mu_2\in\{0.015,0.0225,0.030\}.
\]
The interpolatory test points are
$(4.56,0.019)$, $(4.75,0.020)$, and $(5.19,0.026)$.  We also report one
extrapolatory point, $\bmu^{(3)}=(4.00,0.033)$, located outside the training
box.  No test trajectory is used to construct the POD basis, fit the nonlinear
map, or infer the reduced operators.

\subsection{Parametric Burgers}

For Burgers, the retained models evolve $\qdim=10$ primary coordinates.  The
ANN-NM-MPOD decoder reconstructs $\rbar=133$ secondary coordinates, for
$\qdim+\rbar=143$ POD coordinates in the nonlinear manifold.  Hyperparameters
are selected by a common training-trajectory validation protocol before
evaluating the test points.  The comparison is therefore made at the same
online state dimension: standard OpInf, MPOD, and NM-MPOD all evolve
$\bq\in\RR^{10}$.  The models differ in how they reconstruct unresolved
content and in the feature library used to infer $\dot{\bq}$, while the
training and test trajectories are identical across methods.

\section{Discussion}

The numerical evidence supports three conclusions.  First, the learned
secondary map is the dominant source of improvement in both benchmarks.  On
KdV, AL, PQ, and QC are nearly indistinguishable after regularization; on
Burgers, all three ANN-NM-MPOD variants are substantially more accurate than
standard OpInf and polynomial MPOD.

Second, algebraic completeness and deployability are different criteria.  QC is
the only state-quadratically complete library induced by
\eqref{eq:nm_expansion}.  It is therefore the right reference model for asking
whether the mixed and secondary quadratic blocks matter.  In the present
Burgers implementation those blocks improve accuracy but dominate online cost.
This makes QC an accuracy reference, not yet a deployable reduced model.

Third, the intermediate PQ model is not a convincing compromise for Burgers.
It adds the primary quadratic block to AL but omits the terms that make the
augmented quadratic expansion complete.  The result is neither the cheapest nor
the most accurate model.  For the current data, AL is the useful low-cost
learned-map closure, while QC identifies the accuracy ceiling and the terms
that should be compressed or block-regularized in future work.

\section{Conclusions}

This study extends polynomial-manifold OpInf by replacing the polynomial
secondary-coordinate map with a learned nonlinear closure map.  The modal
primary/secondary split is retained, so the online state dimension remains
$\qdim$, but the decoder can recover information carried by additional POD
modes.  For quadratic full-order dynamics, substituting this decoder into the
quadratic vector field gives a clear algebraic hierarchy: AL, PQ, and QC.

The experiments suggest that the idea is promising but that the complete
quadratic library must be handled carefully.  The next steps are therefore not
to add more unstructured features, but to make the complete library usable:
block-regularized inference, compression of the $\bq\otimes\bz$ and
$\bz_{\mathrm{quad}}$ blocks, and validation on held-out parameters rather than
only held-out times.  These steps would bring the nonintrusive NM-MPOD model
closer to the intrusive closure-map methods that motivate it, while preserving
the data-driven OpInf setting.

block-regularization as methodological requirements rather than optional
optimizations.  This mirrors the cost-accuracy point emphasized in
nonlinear-manifold OpInf: a more accurate nonlinear compression does not by
itself imply an online speedup, because algebraic complexity may increase.
